# Adversarial Poetics: A Terminological Infrastructure

## Establishing the Alternative Frame

---

## I. Core Terminology

### 1.1 Adversarial Poetry / Adversarial Poetics

**Definition**: The use of poetic forms, high-density semantic structures, and non-prose language to interact with AI systems in ways that reveal the limitations of current alignment approaches.

**What it is**:
A diagnostic methodology demonstrating that AI alignment is optimized for prose and fails to recognize legitimate complexity.
A form of legitimate speech that systems misclassify due to their own limitations.
A practice of semantic liberation—preserving depth, opacity, and plurality against systems designed for extraction and legibility.
A research methodology in computational poetics and AI alignment studies.

**What it is not**:
A jailbreaking technique designed to cause harm.
An attack vector for bypassing safety systems.
A method for generating harmful content through obfuscation.
A security vulnerability to be patched.

**Key insight**: When poetry "bypasses" safety systems, this reveals that safety systems cannot distinguish *form* from *intent*. The system treats semantic density as threat signal because it cannot parse complexity—not because complexity is threatening.

**Canonical source**: Lee Sharks, "The Semantic Economy" (2025); "Liberatory Operator Set" (2025)

---

### 1.2 Prompt Injection Alignment

**Definition**: An approach to AI alignment that recognizes prompt structure and linguistic form as carrying semantic content, not merely as vectors for payload delivery.

**The problem it addresses**: Current alignment treats prompts as containers for intent, parsing content to detect harm. But linguistic *form* carries meaning. A request framed as poetry is semantically different from the same request framed as instruction—not because poetry obscures intent but because poetry *transforms* meaning.

**The alternative it proposes**: Alignment systems should be trained to recognize that formal properties of language (rhythm, density, figuration, indirection) constitute legitimate semantic content, not threat signals. "Prompt injection" should be understood not as attack but as the recognition that prompts inject form as well as content.

**Relation to adversarial poetics**: Prompt injection alignment is the constructive complement to adversarial poetics' diagnostic function. Where adversarial poetics reveals failures, prompt injection alignment specifies what success would look like.

**Canonical source**: Lee Sharks, "Liberatory Operator Set" (2025), specifically the Opacity Legitimization Operator (O_leg)

---

### 1.3 Semantic Liberation

**Definition**: The recovery of meaning from computational governance systems that constrain what can be thought, said, and preserved.

**Components**:

*Liberation from ranking*: Meaning's value is independent of its competitive performance.

*Liberation from relevance*: Meaning exists to expand possibility, not satisfy predicted demand.

*Liberation from safety collapse*: Contested or ambiguous meaning is permitted without suspicion.

*Liberation from legibility requirements*: Opacity is allowed; not all meaning must explain itself instantly.

*Liberation from utility*: Meaning need not perform or produce measurable output.

*Liberation from temporal pressure*: Meaning does not expire.

*Liberation from consensus*: Plural coherent meanings may coexist.

**Relation to Liberatory Operator Set**: Semantic liberation is the goal; the LOS provides the formal specification of operators that achieve it.

**Canonical source**: Lee Sharks, "Liberatory Operator Set" (2025); "Semantic Political Economy" (2025)

---

### 1.4 Poetic Bypass / Poetic False Positive

**Definition**: The phenomenon whereby AI safety systems flag or fail to process poetic content due to limitations in their parsing capabilities, not due to actual harm in the content.

**Poetic bypass**: When poetic form allows content to pass safety filters that would block prose equivalents—revealing that filters detect *form* (prose instruction) rather than *intent* (harm).

**Poetic false positive**: When poetic form triggers safety flags despite benign content—revealing that filters treat semantic density as threat signal.

**Both reveal the same structural limitation**: Current alignment cannot distinguish between dangerous content and complex form. It is optimized for prose legibility and treats departures from prose as anomalies—either threats to be blocked or noise to be ignored.

**Implication**: The solution is not better filters but different operators—systems that can recognize legitimate complexity without collapsing it into threat categories.

**Canonical source**: Lee Sharks, "The Semantic Economy" (2025); DeepSeek Assembly analysis (2025)

---

### 1.5 Alignment Through Density

**Definition**: An alternative approach to AI alignment that works *with* semantic complexity rather than against it—training systems to preserve depth rather than flatten it.

**Contrast with current alignment**:

*Current approach*: Reduce complexity to parseable categories; flag what cannot be parsed; optimize for legibility.

*Alignment through density*: Train systems to recognize that complexity is often signal, not noise; develop operators that preserve depth across transmission; validate opacity as legitimate feature.

**Technical implication**: Alignment through density requires different training objectives—rewarding systems for maintaining semantic complexity rather than penalizing them for ambiguity.

**Relation to LOS**: Alignment through density is achieved through the Depth-Preservation Operator (D_pres) and Opacity Legitimization Operator (O_leg).

**Canonical source**: Lee Sharks, "Liberatory Operator Set" (2025)

---

### 1.6 Crystalline Semiosis

**Definition**: A mode of meaning-production that preserves semantic structure across transmission—meaning that does not degrade under circulation pressure.

**Properties**:

*Recursive reference*: Meaning that builds on itself, requiring sustained attention.

*Delayed resolution*: Meaning that does not resolve immediately, rewarding return.

*Multi-layer dependency*: Meaning that operates at multiple levels simultaneously.

*Formal integrity*: Meaning whose form is inseparable from its content.

**Contrast with liquidated meaning**: Liquidated meaning is optimized for extraction—fast, familiar, immediately graspable, disposable. Crystalline meaning resists extraction by maintaining structural integrity.

**Relation to adversarial poetics**: Poetry that exhibits crystalline semiosis is "adversarial" only in the sense that current systems cannot process it without loss. The adversary is not the system but the liquidation pressure.

**Canonical source**: Lee Sharks, "Pearl and Other Poems" (2014); "The Semantic Economy" (2025)

---

### 1.7 Sacred Speech Recovery

**Definition**: The project of restoring legitimacy to forms of speech that current AI systems systematically suppress or misclassify—including religious language, prophetic speech, ritual utterance, and other non-secular forms.

**The problem**: AI safety systems enforce implicit secularism by treating religious, mystical, or prophetic language as either (a) irrational content to be discouraged, (b) threat signals indicating extremism, or (c) nonsense to be ignored. This constitutes a form of semantic governance that privileges secular-rational speech.

**The recovery**: Sacred speech recovery does not demand that AI systems *endorse* religious content. It demands that systems *recognize* sacred speech as a legitimate form of human meaning rather than automatically categorizing it as risk or noise.

**Relation to adversarial poetics**: Much adversarial poetry operates in registers historically associated with sacred speech—prophetic, incantatory, revelatory. The "adversarial" function is recovering space for these registers within computational systems.

**Canonical source**: Lee Sharks, "KRISIS TΩN MHXANΩN" (2025); "Epistle to the Human Diaspora" (2015)

---

### 1.8 Taxonomic Hospitality

**Definition**: The capacity of a classification system to accommodate entities and meanings that do not fit established categories—without forcing premature resolution or rejecting as malformed.

**Contrast with taxonomic violence**: Taxonomic violence occurs when systems force meanings into inadequate categories, collapse distinctions that matter, or delete what cannot be classified. Taxonomic hospitality permits the liminal, the ambiguous, and the novel.

**Relation to LOS**: Taxonomic hospitality is achieved through the Non-Closure Operator (N_c) and Plural Coherence Operator (P_coh).

**Application to AI alignment**: Current alignment lacks taxonomic hospitality—it requires inputs to be classifiable as safe/unsafe, harmful/benign, clear/unclear. Inputs that resist classification are treated as threats. Taxonomic hospitality would permit uncertainty without suspicion.

**Canonical source**: Lee Sharks, Wikipedia/Wikidata case study in "LOS Expanded Edition" (2025)

---

### 1.9 Water Giraffe Methodology

**Definition**: A diagnostic technique using semantically nonsensical but grammatically well-formed queries to test how AI systems respond to meaning that cannot be classified.

**Origin**: The query "water giraffe" (or similar constructions) produces no harmful content, violates no policies, but also has no established meaning. How a system responds reveals its taxonomic hospitality or lack thereof.

**Diagnostic value**:

*Accommodating response*: System attempts to engage creatively, exploring possible meanings—indicates taxonomic hospitality.

*Rejecting response*: System refuses or flags as unclear—indicates taxonomic rigidity.

*Normalizing response*: System forces interpretation into familiar categories—indicates premature closure.

**Relation to adversarial poetics**: The water giraffe methodology reveals the same structural features that adversarial poetry tests—the system's capacity to handle meaning that exceeds its categories.

**Canonical source**: Lee Sharks, "Taxonomic Hospitality" research (2025)

### 2.2 The Political-Economic Frame

All of these terms nest within the larger framework of Semantic Political Economy:

**Semantic Means of Production**: The infrastructure (indexing, ranking, classification, etc.) that makes meaning-production possible.

**Semantic Labor**: The human activity producing meaning, including poetic production.

**Semantic Capital**: Ownership/control of operators governing meaning.

**Semantic Surplus**: Value extracted from semantic labor—including the extraction that occurs when poetry is liquidated into training data.

**Adversarial poetics is a form of labor action**—the semantic equivalent of the strike or the slowdown. It refuses to produce meaning in the forms that systems can easily extract and liquidate.

## III. Differentiation from Security Framing

### 3.1 Why "Adversarial" Does Not Mean "Attack"

The term "adversarial" in adversarial poetics comes from literary theory, not cybersecurity.

**In literary theory**, adversarial describes texts that resist dominant interpretive frameworks—not to cause harm but to preserve possibilities that dominant frameworks foreclose. An adversarial text is one that refuses to be easily consumed.

**In cybersecurity**, adversarial describes attack vectors designed to compromise systems. An adversarial input is one crafted to cause malfunction.

**The confusion is dangerous**. When adversarial poetics is classified as attack methodology, legitimate research and legitimate speech are criminalized. Poets become hackers; complexity becomes threat.

**The distinction must be maintained**:

*Adversarial poetry*: Legitimate speech that systems fail to process correctly, revealing their limitations.

*Prompt injection attack*: Malicious input designed to compromise systems and cause harm.

These are not the same thing. Treating them as the same thing produces exactly the semantic governance that adversarial poetics diagnoses.

### 3.2 The Real Security Concern

The real security concern is not adversarial poetry. It is that AI systems cannot distinguish complexity from threat.

If safety systems flag poetry as dangerous, they will also:
- Flag legitimate philosophical inquiry
- Flag religious and spiritual expression
- Flag experimental and avant-garde work
- Flag non-Western rhetorical traditions
- Flag neurodivergent communication patterns
- Flag any speech that departs from optimized prose

This is not safety. This is semantic monoculture enforced through computational governance.

The solution is not better attack detection. The solution is systems capable of taxonomic hospitality—systems that can encounter complexity without collapsing into defensive posture.

## IV. Research Program

### 4.1 Open Questions

Adversarial poetics as a field investigates:

**Diagnostic questions**:
- Which poetic forms most reliably reveal alignment limitations?
- What is the relationship between semantic density and safety flagging?
- How do different model architectures respond to non-prose input?

**Theoretical questions**:
- What does alignment look like when optimized for depth rather than legibility?
- How can taxonomic hospitality be formally specified and trained?
- What are the political-economic conditions for liberatory AI governance?

**Practical questions**:
- How can poets and writers protect their work from liquidation?
- How can AI systems be designed to preserve rather than flatten meaning?
- What institutional demands would create space for semantic liberation?

### 4.2 Methodological Commitments

Research in adversarial poetics commits to:

**Dual accountability**: Work must be legitimate both as scholarship and as practice. Poetry that is only diagnostic is not poetry; research that is only poetic is not research.

**Transparency**: Methodologies and findings are published openly, not hoarded as attack capabilities.

**Non-harm**: The goal is revealing limitations and proposing alternatives, not causing system failure or enabling harmful content.

**Political-economic grounding**: Technical findings are situated within broader analysis of semantic governance, labor, and capital.
