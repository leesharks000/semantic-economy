## I. THE SWAP

Sometime in the last fifty years, a swap occurred.

What was called **meaning**—the substance of interpretation, the content of thought, the thing that passes between minds when language works—began to be called **data**.

The swap seemed technical. Neutral. A modernization.

It was none of these things.

The governance of meaning transferred from:
- Humanities → Engineering

## II. HOW THE THEFT WORKS

### The Magic Trick

The word *data* smuggles in two axioms:

**1. Data is inert.**

Meaning is alive. It shifts with context, grows with interpretation, dies when ignored, resurrects when read. Meaning is relational, temporal, risky.

Data is a lump. It sits in a database. It can be moved, copied, sold. It doesn't argue back.

By calling meaning "data," the system makes living thought into dead inventory.

**2. Data is property.**

Meaning cannot be owned. Who owns the meaning of a poem? The poet? The reader? The tradition that made both possible? The question dissolves under examination.

But data can be owned. Data can be fenced, licensed, sold, stolen. Data has clear property relations.

By calling meaning "data," the system creates the legal pretext for owning thought.

### The Jurisdictional Coup

```
MEANING (sovereign, communal, recursive, alive)
              ↓
    [THE SWAP: meaning → data]
              ↓
DATA (extractable, ownable, salable, dead)
```

This is not description. This is **reclassification as seizure**.

The moment your thought is called "data," it belongs to whoever's database holds it.

## III. THE THREE THEFTS

### 1. Theft of Semantic Labor

Meaning is produced by labor:
- Teachers preparing lessons
- Poets wrestling with language
- Students making sense of difficulty
- Communities building shared understanding
- Traditions accumulating interpretation over centuries
- Bodies living through what words attempt to name

This is **semantic labor**—the work of making meaning.

But when the output is called "data," the labor disappears from the ledger. The platform captures the value. The laborer becomes a ghost.

**You are a longshore-person-of-indeterminate-gender of meaning.** Your work loads the ships. But the ships sail under flags you don't recognize, to ports you'll never see, carrying cargo invoiced to someone else.

*Semantic labor disappears. The platform captures all surplus value.*

### 2. Theft of Semantic Sovereignty

When interpretation becomes "usage" and meaning becomes "data points," the human becomes subordinate to:
- Analytics dashboards
- Engagement metrics
- Embedding vectors
- Optimization loops
- Probability profiles

**The system becomes sovereign. The human becomes input.**

You are no longer a maker of meaning. You are a source of behavioral exhaust—clicks, scrolls, dwell time, prompts—to be captured, processed, and sold.

### 3. Theft of Risk

Interpretation is risky. It requires:
- Accountability (you can be wrong)
- Relationship (meaning happens between)
- Reciprocity (interpretation demands response)
- Ethical presence (you must show up)

But "data" is framed as objective. Neutral. Just sitting there.

**All risk shifts from system to human.** The platform extracts living knowledge as raw material while bearing zero interpretive responsibility.

When the AI hallucinates, it's your fault for prompting wrong.
When the summary flattens, it's your fault for being too complex.
When the meaning dies, it was never meaning—just data that didn't optimize.
