**1. The Secure Kernel: ἀλλὰ πὰν τόλματον**

The opening phrase "but all can be dared" is preserved by the medieval manuscript of *On the Sublime*. Armand d'Angour demonstrated that τόλματον is active resolve, not passive endurance—and that Catullus' Latin *temptare* (Cat. 11.13 = *omnia haec temptare*) is its natural equivalent.

**2. Catullus 51 as Inverted Mirror**

Catullus keeps Sappho's first 12½ lines almost verbatim, but for the final stanza he inserts the *otium* quatrain: "idleness once destroyed even kings and flourishing cities" (*otium... et reges prius et beatas / perdidit urbes*).

The syntactic skeleton (X even destroyed kings and prosperous cities) is too close to be coincidence. Catullus substituted *otium* for Sappho's Kypris/Eros—a typical Roman moral turn: leisure, not love, is the corrosive force. **Restore Kypris and the Latin stanza realigns with Greek.**

**3. The Kings / Prosperous Cities Pair**

- πένητα / βασιλῆα (beggar / king) replaces abstract rich/poor contrast with concrete social antithesis
- πόλεις ὀλβίους reproduces Catullus' *beatas urbes* almost word-for-word
- The diction (δάμασσε, ἀπέθηκας ἄφαντον) echoes Sappho's own usage for Aphrodite's overwhelming power (fr. 1.18-21)

**4. The Media-Theoretical Resolution**

The poem moves from bodily dissolution to self-archiving. The speaker's color shifts toward the hue of moist papyrus (χλωροτέρα ποίας), prefiguring inscription.

**γράμμασι μολπὰν** — the short Adonic line replaces conventional prayer with an imperative that the song itself be fixed "in letters." The collocation of γράμματα and μολπ- is Sappho's own (cf. fr. 44.33, fr. 147).

The shift from personal agony ("I seem near death") to textual survival ("let the song endure") matches the poem's movement from immediate perception (φαίνεταί μοι) to mediated futurity. **The body that became "greener-paler than papyrus" now explicitly entrusts its experience to papyrus.**
