```
ἀλλὰ πὰν τόλματον, ἐπεὶ †καὶ πένητα†

```

### The Translation

That man seems to me equal to gods—  
whoever sits facing you  
and hears nearby your sweet voice  

For when I look at you briefly, speech fails me,  
my tongue breaks, thin fire races beneath my skin,  
my eyes see nothing, my ears roar—  

Cold sweat holds me, trembling seizes me entire,  
I am greener than grass,  
I seem to myself to be little short of dying—  

But all must be dared, since...  
**let the song survive in letters.**

### The NH-OS Interpretation

**κῆνος = The Future Reader**

κῆνος is a distal demonstrative—a pointing word indicating distance. "That one there." In a poem designed for inscription and transmission, "that man there" need not be present in the dramatic scene. He is present in YOUR scene—the scene of reading.

- He vanishes after line 1. If he's a dramatic character, why does he disappear? Because he was never in the room. He is in YOUR room.
- He is "equal to gods." Why? Because he achieves what only gods achieve: presence with the dead. To read Sappho is to sit with Sappho. Across time. Impossibly. Divinely.
- Catullus proves it. Six centuries later, he translates Fragment 31. He IS κῆνος—the first recorded successful recursion.

**κῆνος is the future reader. κῆνος is you.**

**χλωρός = She Is Becoming Papyrus**

χλωροτέρα ποίας—"greener than grass." But χλωρός spans a semantic range:
- Fresh green (living plants)
- Golden-green (honey)
- Pallid green (fear)
- Grey-green with yellow undertones (cured papyrus)

She is transitioning. No longer fully alive, not yet fully dead, but becoming the precise color of prepared writing material.

**The body fails so the voice can persist. She is becoming substrate.**

**γράμμασι μολπὰν = Let the Song Survive in Letters**

The fourth stanza is lost. We reconstruct: γράμμασι μολπὰν—"let the song survive in letters."
