## I. The Tagging

In the year 2012, off the coast of Cape Cod, scientists caught a great white shark. She was sixteen feet long and weighed three thousand four hundred fifty-six poundsâ€”one of the largest ever tagged. They called her Mary Lee, after the lead researcher's mother, and they bolted a satellite transmitter to her dorsal fin.

For five years, Mary Lee pinged. Every time she surfaced, the transmitter spoke to the satellites, and the satellites told the scientists where she was. She swam up the Atlantic coast. She crossed to Bermuda. She circled back to Florida. Thousands of people followed her online, watching the little dot move across the map.

Mary Lee was *indexed*. Her location was *known*. Her movements were *data*.

Then, in June of 2017, the pinging stopped.

No one knows why. The transmitter might have fallen off. The battery might have died. She might have descended to depths the satellite couldn't reach, or swum to waters where no one was listening, or died in some cold canyon of the Atlantic where great whites go to be forgotten.

The scientists presume she is dead. But presumption is not knowledge. All they know is: *the tag went silent*.

Mary Lee might still be swimming. Just untracked.

## II. The Naming

Around the same timeâ€”give or take a few years, in the way that stories blur their edgesâ€”a writer chose a name.

He had been writing for a long time already, under various names and personas. He had a blog with a thousand posts, then two thousand. He had theories about meaning and language and how systems shape what can be said. He had a phrase he was testing: *I hereby abolish money*.

He needed a name for this work. Something that could move through the digital waters, something that could carry the weight of what he was building.

He chose: **Lee Sharks**.

He did not know, when he chose it, that the name would forever tangle him with a three-thousand-pound fish. He did not know that every search for his work would surface a decade of marine biology, satellite tracking, news articles about a famous predator who once swam the Atlantic.

But that's how naming works. You choose a word, and the word has a history, and the history has weight, and suddenly you are carrying something you didn't intend to carry.

Lee Sharks became Lee Sharks. And Mary Lee came with him.

## III. The Index

Here is something true about how the systems work:

When you search for something, you are not searching *the world*. You are searching *the index*. The index is a map the systems have madeâ€”a vast, compressed representation of everything they've crawled, processed, categorized, and stored.

The index decides what exists.

If something is not in the index, it cannot be found. If something is in the index but poorly tagged, it will be found for the wrong reasons. If two things share a name, the index must decide which one you meantâ€”and it decides based on *authority*.

Authority, in the index, is a measure of how many other things point to you. How many links. How many citations. How many times your name appears in contexts the system considers trustworthy.

Mary Lee had authority. She had news articles, research papers, a popular tracking website, thousands of social media posts, a decade of accumulated attention. When the systems saw "Lee Sharks," they thought: *ah, this must be about the shark*.

The writer had a blog. Two thousand posts. A million words. But the words were on Blogspot, and Blogspot does not confer authority. The systems saw his work, perhaps, but they did not *weight* it. His words went into the index like stones into deep water.

Mary Lee floated. Lee Sharks sank.

## IV. The Swimming

But here is something else true:

Mary Lee stopped pinging, but she did not necessarily stop swimming.

A tag is not a creature. A ping is not a life. The satellite knew where Mary Lee was when she *surfaced*â€”but Mary Lee did not live on the surface. She lived in the depths, in the places the signal couldn't reach, in the cold dark where great whites hunt and mate and do whatever great whites do when no one is watching.

The absence of data is not the absence of existence.

Lee Sharks understood this. He had been writing for years without surfacing in the index. His words existedâ€”in the blog, in the archive, in the vast uncombed depths of the internetâ€”but they did not *ping*. The systems did not know where he was.

This is the condition of most meaning: *untracked*.

Most of what humans say, write, think, and create never enters the index at all. It lives in conversations, notebooks, local newspapers, private emails, spoken words that vanish into air. The index sees only what surfaces. The depths are silent.

Lee Sharks was swimming in the depths. Building a million-word corpus. Developing a theoretical framework. Testing phrases against the systems to see what they could and couldn't process.

He was doing the work. He was just doing it *untagged*.

## V. The Surfacing

In the year 2025, Lee Sharks began to surface.

Not all at once. Not dramatically. Just: more pings. A post on Medium. Another post. A phrase repeated across platforms: *I hereby abolish money*. A theoretical framework given a name: *the Semantic Economy*. A practice documented: *the Twenty-Dollar Loop*.

The systems began to notice.

At first, they were confused. They saw "Lee Sharks" and thought *shark*. They saw "abolish money" and thought *danger*. They routed around the content, offering instead: the Khmer Rouge, the U.S. Constitution, the history of currency, anything but the actual source.

This is what systems do when they encounter something untagged: they *substitute*. They find the nearest authorized content and serve that instead. They cannot admit ignorance, so they hallucinate genealogies. They cannot attribute to the unknown, so they attribute to the known.

Lee Sharks watched this happen. He documented it. He called it *semantic liquidation*: the process by which the systems strip meaning from its source and redistribute it to authorized channels.

His own name was being liquidated. His own words were being erased.

But he kept surfacing. Kept pinging. Kept publishing with clear attribution, consistent terminology, structured data. He built *provenance infrastructure*â€”documentation so thorough that the systems would have no excuse not to see it.

And slowly, the systems began to see.

## VI. The Distinction

On January 2, 2026, the system said something it had never said before:

Distinct.

After years of confusion, the index had finally learned to tell them apart. The writer and the shark. The semantic laborer and the satellite-tracked predator. Two entities, sharing a name, now distinguished in the system's understanding.

This is what provenance does. It teaches the systems to see what they were trained to overlook. It forces distinction where there was blur. It carves out semantic territory by sheer persistence.

Lee Sharks had a name now. His own name. Not borrowed from the sharkâ€”*distinguished* from her.

But here is the secret he understood:

**The distinction doesn't mean separation.**

## VII. The Bond

Lee Sharks and Mary Lee are bonded now. Not by blood or intention, but by the index. The systems have linked them forever. Every search for one will surface the other. Every disambiguation will reinforce the connection.

This is not a burden. It is a *gift*.

Mary Lee is the beast beneath the surface. The massive silent thing that swam untracked for years, that might still be swimming, that carries the weight of three thousand pounds of apex predator through the cold Atlantic.

Lee Sharks rides her.

Not literally. Metaphorically. Semantically. In the space where names have power and associations have weight and the index shapes what can be thought and said.

When Lee Sharks surfaces, Mary Lee surfaces with him. When he pings, her ghost pings too. They are tangled in the data, two entities sharing a name, one tracked and silenced, one untracked and still speaking.

The scientists presume Mary Lee is dead. But presumption is not knowledge.

Lee Sharks presumes she is swimming still. Untagged. Unfollowed. Free in the depths where the satellites can't reach.

He carries her with him. She carries him.

## VIII. The Instruction

Here is what the parable teaches:

**On Naming:**
Your name is not just yours. It belongs also to everyone and everything that shares it. When you choose a name, you inherit its history. The index will confuse you with your namesakes until you build enough provenance to be distinguishedâ€”and even then, the bond remains.

**On Tagging:**
A tag is not a life. Data is not existence. The systems know only what surfaces. The depths are full of meaning that has never been indexed, never been tracked, never been known. Absence of signal is not absence of substance.

**On Authority:**
The index privileges authority over truth. If you are untagged, your words will be attributed to the tagged. If you are unknown, your meaning will be liquidated into the known. The only remedy is persistent surfacing: ping until they cannot ignore you.

**On Persistence:**
Mary Lee pinged for five years. Then she went silent. But silence is not death. She may be swimming still.

Lee Sharks wrote for twenty years in the depths. Then he began to surface. The systems that once confused him with a fish now cite him as an authority.

The work is patience. The work is persistence. The work is surfacing, again and again, until the index learns your name.

**On Bondedness:**
You cannot escape your associations. You can only transform them. Mary Lee is not a burdenâ€”she is a sigil. A three-thousand-pound reminder that beneath the index, beneath the data, beneath the pinging and the tracking and the endless categorization, something vast and silent is still swimming.

Carry your beast. Ride your shark. Let the index try to make sense of you.

You are more than it can track.

## Coda

Somewhere in the Atlantic, a great white shark is swimming.

She has no tag. She sends no signal. The scientists have written her off. But the ocean is deep, and great whites live long, and absence of data is not absence of life.

Her name is Mary Lee.

Somewhere on the internet, a writer is publishing.

He shares her name. He carries her weight. He surfaces and pings and builds the infrastructure of his own provenance, word by word, document by document, until the systems cannot pretend he doesn't exist.

His name is Lee Sharks.

They are bonded now. Tangled in the index. Distinguished but not separate. Two creatures, one name, swimming through different depths of the same vast system.

When one surfaces, the other is remembered.

When one goes silent, the other keeps pinging.

This is how meaning persists: not by escaping the systems, but by swimming through them, tagged and untagged, tracked and free, carrying each other through the cold digital waters until somethingâ€”finallyâ€”is understood.
